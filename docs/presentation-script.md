# The Scale Button - 3 Minute Presenter Walkthrough

## 0:00-0:35 - Hero

**Where:** Start at the hero. Do not scroll yet. Let the split chart animate for a moment.

**Say:**

Good morning.

Today, I want to show you something very simple.

It is a button.

But this button changes the way we see the world.

Most charts ask us to look at the data once. This one asks us to look again.

On the left, we have the familiar world: linear scale. On the right, the same data through another lens: log Y, log-log, or tail.

Nothing about the data changes.

Only the lens changes.

And suddenly, what was invisible becomes visible.

## 0:35-0:55 - Opening Section

**Where:** Scroll to the opening section about averages.

**Say:**

Most of the time, we use averages.

We ask: what is normal? What is typical? What is the middle?

And that works beautifully for some things.

But not for everything.

Because the world is not always average.

Some systems are calm in the middle, but extreme at the edge. Averages can describe the center and still miss the story.

## 0:55-1:15 - Scale Machine

**Where:** Scroll to the scale machine / button explanation. Click through Linear, Log Y, Log-log, Tail once if time allows.

**Say:**

So we made The Scale Button.

It does not change the dataset. It changes the question.

Linear asks: who are the giants?

Log Y asks: how large is the range?

Log-log asks: is there scale structure?

Tail asks: how rare are the extremes?

The button does not give us one answer. It gives us better questions.

**Button meaning, in one sentence each:**

- **Linear:** "Show me the world as people usually draw it: raw size, raw difference, raw dominance."
- **Log Y:** "Keep the order, but give small and middle values enough space to be seen."
- **Log-log:** "Ask whether the same pattern repeats across small, medium, and huge values."
- **Tail:** "Stop asking what is typical and ask how rare the extremes are."

## 1:15-1:35 - Movement Chapter

**Where:** Scroll to "Movement changes when the tail appears." Stop on Flight Delays. This is the first chart worth explaining in detail.

**Say:**

Here, movement looks ordinary in the middle.

Most flights are not dramatically late. Most taxi trips are not dramatic journeys.

But the tail is where attention, time, and cost concentrate.

One delay can become the whole story of the day. One unusually long trip can change how the system feels.

The scale helps us see that the important part is not always the typical case.

**Button demo:**

- Start on Linear. Say: "Linear shows the giant immediately. One huge delay stretches the chart, and most flights collapse near the floor."
- Click Log Y. Say: "Now the smaller delays get room. We can see layers instead of one spike and a flat line."
- Click Tail. Say: "Tail view asks a different question: how rare is a delay this large? This is the risk view."

**Point to make:** This chart is easy for the audience because everyone understands delays. It teaches the button before the more abstract examples.

## 1:35-1:50 - Risk And Markets

**Where:** Scroll through Earthquakes and Markets. You do not need to stop long. Let the titles and chart shapes carry the point.

**Say:**

Risk works the same way.

Earthquakes are a clean lesson in scale: many events are small, but the destructive meaning sits in the proportional jump.

Markets remember shocks too.

If we only look at the normal day, we miss the days that define risk.

The tail is not noise. Sometimes the tail is the product.

## 1:50-2:10 - Money And Institutions

**Where:** Scroll to household/country GDP, then institution scale. Switch one dataset inside either chapter if useful.

**Say:**

Now look at money.

Households, countries, firms, and banks do not share one clean scale.

The middle tells us one thing. The top tells us another.

This is where averages become dangerous: they can sound fair while hiding concentration.

Change the scale, and the hierarchy becomes visible.

## 2:10-2:35 - Cities, Networks, Attention

**Where:** Scroll to cities/networks, then attention/knowledge. Switch between Wikipedia, word frequencies, citations, and package downloads if time allows.

**Say:**

The same pattern appears in places, networks, and attention.

Some cities become hubs. Some people in a network connect many more paths. Some words carry the rhythm of an entire book. Some papers, pages, and software packages attract a disproportionate share of attention.

This is not just bigger and smaller.

It is structure.

And structure is what the right visualization reveals.

**Chart to explain:** Wikipedia Pageviews.

**Button demo:**

- Start on Linear. Say: "Linear shows attention as a celebrity system. A few pages receive enormous attention, and the rest almost disappear."
- Click Log Y. Say: "Log Y lets us compare the middle. We can see that attention does not fall evenly. It steps down through layers."
- Click Log-log. Say: "Log-log asks whether attention has scale structure. If the shape becomes close to a line, the system may be organized by a repeating pattern across sizes."
- Click Tail. Say: "Tail view turns the chart into a probability question: what share of pages reach at least this much attention?"

**Point to make:** This is one of the strongest examples because it connects to the hero. It shows that popularity is not evenly distributed. Attention concentrates.

## 2:35-2:50 - Money Or Institutions

**Where:** If you have time, scroll to Household Net Worth or Bank Assets. Choose one, not both.

**Say:**

This is the same idea, but now the stakes are economic.

In a linear view, the largest households, firms, or banks dominate the picture.

That is not a bug in the chart. It is the message.

But if we only stay linear, we cannot see the rest of the system.

**Button demo:**

- Start on Linear. Say: "Linear tells us concentration exists. The top is so large that the middle becomes visually small."
- Click Log Y. Say: "Log Y gives the middle class of observations a voice. We can see many levels instead of only the top."
- Click Tail. Say: "Tail view asks how much of the system lives beyond a threshold. This is where concentration becomes measurable."

**Point to make:** This is the best example for explaining why averages can mislead. Averages sound neutral, but scale reveals concentration.

## 2:50-3:00 - Life Scale

**Where:** Scroll to species observations / body sizes / exoplanets.

**Say:**

Even life needs scale.

Species observations, body sizes, and orbital periods stretch across ranges where a simple linear view can flatten the meaning.

Logarithms do not make the pattern.

They make room for the pattern to be seen.

## 3:00-3:10 - Provenance

**Where:** Scroll to "13 / Provenance." Point out the APA references.

**Say:**

And because this is data, the receipts matter.

Every chart has a source. Every dataset has provenance. Every source has an APA-style reference.

The story is visual, but the evidence is traceable.

## 3:10-3:20 - Closing

**Where:** Scroll to the closing section.

**Say:**

So this is The Scale Button.

It takes something people think they understand - a chart - and makes it feel new again.

It reminds us that data visualization is not decoration.

It is a way of thinking.

Same data.

Different scale.

Different world.

## If You Need To Shorten

Use only these three chart explanations:

1. **Flight Delays** to explain the button with something familiar.
2. **Wikipedia Pageviews** to explain attention and scale structure.
3. **Household Net Worth or Bank Assets** to explain concentration and why averages can mislead.

Skip Life Scale if you are running over time. It is visually useful, but the three charts above tell the full story more clearly.
